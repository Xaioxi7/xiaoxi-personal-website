Match and Mismatch Between Perceived Safety and System Assessment in Autonomous Driving

Xiaoxi Wang — Research Proposal

BELIV Lab · Arizona State University

1. Research Question and Hypotheses

What factors drive matches and mismatches between human perceived safety and autonomous vehicle safety assessments, and what insights can these alignments and gaps provide for AV system design?

Hypotheses:

  1. When a real safety hazard exists, humans can accurately perceive and judge the risk, while the autonomous vehicle system may fail to detect it. Humans may identify risks through specific environmental cues, visual information, or contextual reasoning that the current system may not capture.
  2. When a real safety hazard exists, the autonomous system can correctly identify the risk, but humans may fail to perceive it, misjudge it, or experience attention blind spots. This situation may result from limitations in human perception or may be caused by insufficient or unclear information delivery through vehicles' HMI.
  3. When no actual safety hazard exists, humans can correctly judge the situation as safe, while the system may falsely detect a risk. Humans may rule out false dangers by integrating comprehensive contextual information, which the system may learn from to reduce false alarms and unnecessary disturbance to users.

2. Literature Review

From an engineering perspective, existing research has proposed different safety evaluation methods for autonomous driving. Wishart et al. (2025) proposed a scenario-level evaluation method based on dynamic driving tasks, allowing the system to generate quantifiable safety metrics across different driving scenarios. Wishart et al. (2023) further proposed a safety case framework for automated vehicles from the perspectives of system design and scenario-based testing. Together, these studies provide an important engineering baseline for evaluating autonomous driving safety.

From a human perspective, existing research shows that users' risk perception and trust in autonomous driving systems can be examined through behavioral responses, physiological signals, and multimodal data. He et al. (2022) showed that human judgments of traffic risk can be predicted through visual features. Based on this, in addition to traditional interviews and surveys, multimodal methods such as eye-tracking, electrodermal activity, real-time voice responses, and button-based feedback recording (Deng et al., 2024) can help researchers better understand user states and cognitive load.

A key issue in autonomous driving research is that engineering safety does not always align with passengers' perceived safety. Current autonomous systems mainly rely on objective indicators such as following distance, speed, acceleration, and obstacle detection, but these measures may not fully reflect passengers' subjective experience and physiological responses during the ride. As Endsley (2017) pointed out, even as systems become increasingly automated, humans cannot simply be removed; instead, they take on the roles of supervisors, monitors, and interveners.

3. Research Plan and Methods

This study uses driving simulation experiments to collect data on safety judgments made by human passengers and autonomous vehicle (AV) systems in the same scenarios.

3.1 Engineering Safety Assessment

Based on existing engineering safety assessment methods, autonomous driving scenarios will be used to analyze system safety. For each scenario, the AV system will generate safety assessment results, and objective indicators such as following distance, relative speed, obstacle detection, and overall vehicle condition will be recorded.

3.2 Passenger Subjective Experience and Multimodal Data Collection

While participants experience the ride as passengers, multimodal data will be collected. Eye-tracking will record visual attention, gaze shifts, and pupil changes. A skin conductance device will capture changes in tension levels. The experiment will also be video recorded to document behavioral responses and facial expressions. Some participants will use a real-time feedback button to indicate when they feel unsafe.

3.3 Scenario Alignment and Matching Analysis

After data collection, physiological signals, behavioral observations, and subjective feedback will be aligned with the vehicle's safety assessment outputs and actual driving events. Matching and mismatching patterns among system judgments, passenger perceptions, and real driving conditions will then be examined.

3.4 Mismatch Analysis and Integrated Evaluation Approach

Based on the alignment results, this study will further examine the causes of matching and mismatching cases. It will explore which scenario characteristics, system behaviors, or passenger response patterns are more likely to lead to agreement or disagreement between system assessment and human perception.

4. Fulton Research Theme

This study belongs to the Data research theme within the Fulton Schools. This study integrates multimodal data and system outputs to explore how these data can be analyzed and utilized to understand differences between users and systems. This study reflects the application of data-driven approaches in intelligent systems and human factors engineering.

Personal Statement

I am currently pursuing a degree in Human Systems Engineering at Arizona State University, where I have developed a strong interest in automotive driving, autonomous systems, and human-computer interaction. I have always believed that human factors and technology should not be separated but should work together. No matter how advanced a product is, it is ultimately designed for people. Therefore, how people understand a system, how they feel about it, and whether they trust it are all essential.

My interest in autonomous driving comes from its close connection to everyday life. Transportation affects almost everyone, and driving is a high-frequency activity for most people. Autonomous driving has the potential to change how people live, but not everyone fully accepts or uses it yet. I believe one important reason is that engineering safety alone is not enough. Even if a system performs well according to technical standards, users may still feel uncertain or uncomfortable if they do not understand it.

Familiarization Assignment: Honeywell Lecture Report

HSE 225 — Arizona State University

Major Challenges Encountered

Aircraft already have a lot of data. They collect information about aircraft systems, flight conditions, life support, and more. And the aircraft itself is already partly automated. But compared to all of that, there is much less data about the pilot. That is the core problem this project is trying to solve. Because the pilot's physical condition, breathing, heart rate, cognitive state, and workload all directly affect their decisions, and their decisions affect the entire flight. So, the question is: how do we make the pilot's own condition something that can also be seen, measured, and understood?

The team built a wearable biosensing garment, essentially a smart shirt or sports bra with embedded sensors that can measure ECG, heart rate, breathing, breathing depth, and movement. But getting there was not simple. They ran into three major challenges along the way.

Challenge 1: Would it be part of the workflow?

The first thing the team did was not run an experiment. The first thing they did was ask pilots whether they would be willing to wear the device. Would it interfere with the harness, the G-suit, the parachute, or the life support system? Who would manage the data? Who would hand out the device and download the data afterward? This matters because a product cannot exist on its own. It always must work together with real people, real tasks, and real environments. If it does not fit into the actual workflow, then even if the experiment works perfectly, it will still be very hard to use in the real world. That is why this step came first.

Challenge 2: Does It Actually Measure Accurately?

The second challenge was whether the device could collect reliable data. Just because a shirt has sensors does not mean the data is correct. Pilots are not sitting still. They are in high-G conditions, thermal chambers, centrifuges, and real flight environments. So, the team compared the data from the wearable against standard medical sensors — traditional ECG and breathing monitors — to see how well they matched. The overall results were good. But some sensors performed less reliably in certain conditions. That is an important finding, because it shows that not every instrument produces accurate data in every situation, and proving that the data is trustworthy is itself a major challenge.

Challenge 3: Can the Data Actually Tell Us Something?

The third challenge was whether this data could tell us if something was wrong with the pilot. The team did not start by immediately trying to detect danger. Instead, they first defined what different breathing patterns look like — what is normal breathing, what is shallow breathing, what is hyperventilation, and what is breath holding. Only after building that standard did they use it to interpret pilot state. This is an important logic. You cannot define the result first and then force the data to match it. You need a standard first, and then you use that standard to understand what the data is saying.

HSI Pillars Covered

Human Factors Engineering

The whole project is built around whether the device fits how pilots work. Not just whether it collects data, but whether it can exist inside the pilot's real environment without getting in the way. That is human factors engineering — does it fit the human, not just the task.

Training

Pilots need to learn how to wear it, maintain it, and understand what the alerts mean. That is a real training requirement.

Health Hazards

The point of this project is to monitor the pilot's physical condition — like breathing, heart rate, signs of hypoxia or fatigue — and catch a problem before it affects the flight. So, system safety and health hazard monitoring are basically what the shirt is for.

Human Requirements

First, it must be comfortable and not interfere with existing gear. Pilots already have a harness, G-suit, oxygen system. If this shirt causes any conflict with those, it simply will not get used.

Second, the whole data process needs to fit into the existing workflow. Who gives out the device, who downloads it afterward — if that is unclear or adds too many steps, it will not work in practice.

And the most important one: the pilot still must be the one making decisions. Right now, this system can send an alert, but it cannot tell you exactly what is wrong or what to do. The final call stays with the human.

How This Relates to HSI Practices

What I think is most important about this lecture is not just what the team built. It is how they approached building it.

They did not start with technology. They started with the people. Before they ran any experiments, they asked whether pilots would use the device and whether it could fit into the real environment. That is the most important step, and it is also the step that a lot of projects skip. A product cannot just work in a lab. It must work in the real world, with real people, in real conditions.

Then the next step was proving whether the product is useful, not just technically, but from the human side. Data is not automatically valuable. Just because a shirt can collect physiological signals does not mean those signals are telling us something real. The team had to validate the data against medical-grade sensors and prove the readings were reliable under real flight conditions.

And then the most important logic of all — if you want to use data to explain human state, you cannot define the result first and then force the data to match. You need to build a standard first — define what different signals mean — and then use that standard to interpret the data. That is what the team did when they defined different breathing patterns before trying to assess pilot condition. It is human centered. And that is why I think this project is one of the clearest examples of HSI in practice.

HSI Requirements Generation and Evaluation of a Product

Oura Ring

HSE 225 — Arizona State University

Part 1: Introduction

1.1 Goal Definition

Oura Ring is a smart health monitoring wearable designed to help users track their physiological data through continuous sensing. The device collects data in a visualized format through a companion mobile application, allowing users to monitor health metrics, receive early warnings of potential health risks, and make improvements to their daily habits and overall wellbeing. The device provides tracking across more than 50 health indicators, including sleep quality, heart rate, body temperature, blood oxygen saturation, daily activity levels, stress levels, cardiovascular health, and respiratory rate.

1.2 User Description

Oura Ring is primarily designed for everyday consumers, who make up more than 95% of its user base. A small portion of users come from commercial applications, including corporate wellness programs, healthcare institutions, and military organizations, accounting for approximately 5% of total usage. The US military is currently Oura's largest enterprise client, with tens of thousands of service members using the ring for fatigue monitoring.

Oura Ring's core user group consists of individuals between the ages of 25 and 44 who are highly educated, have disposable income, live in urban areas, and have a strong awareness of their personal health. By June 2024, female users surpassed male users for the first time, with women accounting for 59% and men accounting for 41% of the user base.

Part 2: Requirements Definition

This section covers six HSI pillars: Human Factors Engineering, Personnel, Training, Health Hazard, System Safety, and Survivability. A total of 11 requirements are defined below.

Human Factors Engineering

Requirement 1: Wearability and Physical Comfort

Oura Ring requires users to wear it continuously throughout the day and night, including during sleep, exercise, and everyday activities. The finger is one of the most frequently used parts of the human body, and any discomfort can directly affect normal daily behavior. Continuous wear is essential for ensuring uninterrupted data collection and effective health monitoring.

Threshold: The Oura Ring shall meet the following four criteria: weight shall not exceed 6 grams; after 24 hours of continuous wear, the System Usability Scale comfort score shall be no lower than 68 out of 100; at least 78% of users shall report no longer being noticeably aware of the ring after 2 hours of wear; at least 78% of users shall report that the ring does not interfere with their daily activities.

Objective: The Oura Ring should meet the following targets: weight should not exceed 5.2 grams; after 7 days of continuous wear, the SUS comfort score should be no lower than 80 out of 100; at least 90% of users should report being able to completely ignore the presence of the ring.

Requirement 2: Application Interface Usability

Users can only access all their health data through the app. The app must allow users to get started quickly, with all features easy to locate and the overall navigation intuitive.

Threshold: New users shall be able to complete device pairing, account setup, and viewing of their first health report within 20 minutes without consulting a manual. Task completion rate shall be no lower than 78%. The overall app SUS score shall be no lower than 68 out of 100.

Requirement 3: Size Accommodation Range

Finger sizes vary significantly across individuals, and the range of available sizes directly determines how broad a user population the product can serve.

Threshold: The Oura Ring shall offer at least 10 size options covering the 5th to 95th percentile finger circumference range of the adult population.

Personnel

Requirement 4: Multi-User Group Adaptability

Oura Ring serves users across a wide range of ages, occupations, and technical skill levels. Both the device and the app must adapt to the actual usage needs of these different groups.

Threshold: The Oura Ring shall support normal use by individuals aged 18 to 75. The app interface shall comply with WCAG 2.1 Level AA accessibility standards.

Health Hazard

Requirement 7: Skin Contact Safety

Oura Ring is in direct and continuous contact with the skin over extended periods of time. The ring's materials may cause allergic reactions, skin irritation, or dermatitis.

Threshold: The Oura Ring shall pass ISO 10993-1 biocompatibility testing standards, including skin irritation and sensitization testing under ISO 10993-10.

System Safety

Requirement 9: Device Temperature Control

Oura Ring contains a lithium-ion battery that generates heat during operation. In December 2025, multiple users reported incidents of overheating and emitting smoke, with at least one user sustaining a blister from the heat on their finger.

Threshold: Under all normal operating conditions, the outer surface temperature of the Oura Ring shall not exceed 43 degrees Celsius, in compliance with IEC 62368-1 consumer electronics thermal safety standards.

Part 3: Evaluation Documentation

Requirement 1 Evaluation: Wearability and Physical Comfort

Evaluation Result: Oura Ring 4 official data lists the weight range as 3.3 to 5.2 grams, meeting the threshold of 6 grams. User reviews indicate that most users pass an initial adjustment period of approximately 3 to 5 days before the ring becomes unnoticeable. On the Best Buy platform, the ring received a comfort score of 4.7 out of 5 across 3,685 reviews, with 93% of users willing to recommend it to a friend. Conclusion: Threshold met.

Requirement 2 Evaluation: Application Interface Usability

Evaluation Result: The Oura App interface is clean, and user feedback is generally positive. New users are typically able to complete the basic setup within 10 to 15 minutes, roughly meeting the threshold. However, a significant usability failure exists in subscription management — the cancellation option is buried in the web-based account portal rather than being accessible within the app itself, leading to unintended ongoing charges. Conclusion: Basic interface usability meets the threshold, but subscription management does not.

Requirement 9 Evaluation: Device Temperature Control

Evaluation Result: Under normal use conditions, the ring's outer surface temperature remains close to body temperature and has not been observed to exceed the safety threshold. However, overheating incidents reported by users in December 2025 demonstrate that in rare cases, lithium battery thermal runaway can occur. Oura classified these as isolated incidents and launched an investigation. Conclusion: Normal use meets the threshold, but a safety risk exists in extreme cases.

3.2 Conclusion

Oura Ring performs well across its core requirements. Based on 3,685 real user reviews on Best Buy, the ring received a rating of 4.7 out of 5, with 93% of users willing to recommend it. Positive feedback consistently highlights accurate sleep tracking, comfortable and lightweight wear, long battery life, a discreet appearance like a regular ring, and useful health insights. Negative feedback centers on the ring feeling slightly bulky, subscription payment difficulties, and occasional sizing challenges.

Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Implement a proactive data reliability alert system. Oura Ring currently has no mechanism to inform users when data accuracy has decreased due to sweat, ring displacement, or poor sensor contact. Users may make health decisions based on data they believe to be accurate, but which is in fact compromised.

Recommendation 2: Improve subscription design to lower user barriers. The current subscription model has several transparency problems. The free trial requires a linked payment card, and billing begins automatically after the trial ends. The cancellation option is only accessible through the web account portal rather than within the app itself. These issues have generated large volumes of user complaints and led to class action litigation in late 2025.

How Humans Perceive Humanoid Robot Movement

HSE 323 — Arizona State University

When people look at robotics, especially humanoid robotics, they very easily take "more human-like" as a sign of success. If it looks more human, people often think it is more advanced, more successful, or simply better designed. But the original point, or the real point, of robotics is not to look human. The point of robotics is to complete tasks and, more importantly, to serve humans.

No matter if it is in living scenarios, companion robotics, service robotics, or industrial scenarios, the core is the same: robots are interacting with humans. So the key is not only movement, and it is not only function. The key is interaction. And once the key becomes interaction, then the key becomes understanding, prediction, and trust. People need to understand what the robot is doing, predict what it will do next, and decide whether they can trust it.

That is why robotics design, especially humanoid robotics design, always pays attention to user experience, human factors, and human perception. Because for robotics, moving is not enough. A robot can move, but that does not mean people understand it. A robot can complete an action, but that does not mean people trust it.

And this is also why humanoid robotics keeps trying to become more human-like. Even though uncanny valley was already proposed a long time ago, people are still trying to design robots that look more and more human. The reason is simple. Humans are most familiar with themselves. You may not be able to describe exactly how each part of the body moves, or what the exact movement limit is, but when you see movement, you immediately know whether it feels human or not.

But robots are still machines. Their systems are built for function, and their systems are fundamentally different from humans. Robots are electrically driven. Humans are biologically driven. Humans move with muscles, joints, tendons, and body coordination. Robots move with motors, actuators, controllers, and mechanical constraints. So even if a robot looks human, it still cannot move exactly like a human. And this creates the problem. When something looks human, people expect it to move like a human.

And this problem shows up especially clearly in robot arms and hands. Humans use their arms and hands constantly, and humans also watch arms and hands constantly. Upper-limb movement is visually present all the time. People spend a lot of time watching hands — hands reaching, turning, pointing, touching, holding, and grasping. So people are extremely familiar with hand and arm movement, including trajectory, speed, timing, and gesture. That is why even a very small unusual thing — like a trajectory that is too straight, movement that is too stiff, or motion that starts too suddenly — can immediately feel wrong.

How Humans Understand Movement

When people see something move, they immediately begin to judge what it is, whether it is alive, what it is trying to do, and whether it will affect them. So human perception of movement is not passive observation. It is a very active and very sensitive judgment system.

Humans are naturally very sensitive to movement because movement itself carries meaning. Humans do not only observe movement. Humans naturally try to interpret movement as intention. Even simple motion can be read as chasing, avoiding, hesitating, approaching, or threatening. This is important because it means robot movement is never just motion. Once something moves in front of a person, that movement is already being interpreted.

This is also why biological motion is so important. Humans are extremely sensitive to biological motion. Even if people only see a few moving points in darkness, they can still tell whether it is a person walking, running, reaching, or turning. This shows that humans do not need full form to understand movement. Humans can read movement patterns directly.

Problem 1: The Body Part Looks Human, But Does Not Feel Human

The first problem is when a robotic body part looks human, but does not feel human. For example, it has a palm, five fingers, finger joints, and maybe even skin-colored soft material. It looks like a hand. But when people get closer, they notice the surface is too smooth, too shiny, too cold, too hard, or too rubber-like.

This is what Poliakoff et al. (2018) discussed in their mismatch hypothesis. They showed that when an artificial body part gives some cues that look human, but other cues do not match human expectation, people can feel uneasy. It is not exactly fear. It is more like something feels wrong. And this is also why a fully mechanical industrial arm usually does not create the same reaction. It is clearly a machine. People do not expect it to feel human.

Problem 2: Looks Human, But Does Not Move Human

The second problem is more obvious. It already gives people the expectation that it is human-like, but when it moves, it does not move like a human.

Wu et al. (2024) showed exactly this point. More anthropomorphic robots can attract more attention, but they can also create more discomfort and uncanny feeling when their appearance and behavior do not match. A human does not simply raise an arm in a straight constant-speed line. Before movement starts, there is usually preparation. The eyes move first. The head may slightly turn. The shoulder adjusts. Then the shoulder joint leads the upper arm, the elbow follows to extend or bend, the wrist adjusts orientation, and the fingers prepare for the target. Human movement is not one joint moving. It is coordinated movement.

But many humanoid robots do not move like this. They start directly. The arm rises in a straight line at constant speed. There is no preparation, no natural coordination, and no gradual transition. So people do not feel like they are watching a human-like arm move. They feel like they are watching a machine execute a command.

Problem 3: Movement Is Not Continuous

The last problem is continuity. A lot of robot movement feels wrong not because the robot fails, but because the movement is not continuous. It starts too suddenly, stops too suddenly, changes speed too abruptly, or different joints move out of sync.

Human motion perception depends on continuity. Humans naturally connect what they saw one moment ago with what they see the next moment. So when movement is broken, people immediately feel it. From an engineering view, the robot may still complete the task. But from a human view, the movement feels unnatural, uncomfortable, and harder to trust.

Conclusion

When humanoid robotics make people feel strange, sometimes it is not because they are not advanced enough, not flexible enough, or not human-like enough. The key problem is often much simpler: they move the wrong way. The key is not only whether the robot can move, and not only whether it can complete the task. If a robot is meant to interact with humans, then movement must match human perception. Not only function, but matching. Not only movement, but movement that matches how humans naturally see, predict, and understand motion.

Major Challenges Encountered

Aircraft already have a lot of data. They collect information about aircraft systems, flight conditions, life support, and more. And the aircraft itself is already partly automated. But compared to all of that, there is much less data about the pilot. That is the core problem this project is trying to solve. Because the pilot's physical condition, breathing, heart rate, cognitive state, and workload all directly affect their decisions, and their decisions affect the entire flight. So, the question is: how do we make the pilot's own condition something that can also be seen, measured, and understood?

The team built a wearable biosensing garment, essentially a smart shirt or sports bra with embedded sensors that can measure ECG, heart rate, breathing, breathing depth, and movement. But getting there was not simple. They ran into three major challenges along the way.

Challenge 1: Would it be part of the workflow?

The first thing the team did was not run an experiment. The first thing they did was ask pilots whether they would be willing to wear the device. Would it interfere with the harness, the G-suit, the parachute, or the life support system? Who would manage the data? Who would hand out the device and download the data afterward? This matters because a product cannot exist on its own. It always must work together with real people, real tasks, and real environments. If it does not fit into the actual workflow, then even if the experiment works perfectly, it will still be very hard to use in the real world. That is why this step came first.

Challenge 2: Does It Actually Measure Accurately?

The second challenge was whether the device could collect reliable data. Just because a shirt has sensors does not mean the data is correct. Pilots are not sitting still. They are in high-G conditions, thermal chambers, centrifuges, and real flight environments. So, the team compared the data from the wearable against standard medical sensors traditional ECG and breathing monitors to see how well they matched. The overall results were good. But some sensors performed less reliably in certain conditions. That is an important finding, because it shows that not every instrument produces accurate data in every situation, and proving that the data is trustworthy is itself a major challenge.

Challenge 3: Can the Data Actually Tell Us Something?

The third challenge was whether this data could tell us if something was wrong with the pilot. The team did not start by immediately trying to detect danger. Instead, they first defined what different breathing patterns look like what is normal breathing, what is shallow breathing, what is hyperventilation, and what is breath holding. Only after building that standard did they use it to interpret pilot state. This is an important logic. You cannot define the result first and then force the data to match it. You need a standard first, and then you use that standard to understand what the data is saying.

HSI Pillars Covered

Human Factors Engineering

The whole project is built around whether the device fits how pilots work. Not just whether it collects data, but whether it can exist inside the pilot's real environment without getting in the way. That is human factors engineering, does it fit the human, not just the task.

Training

Pilots need to learn how to wear it, maintain it, and understand what the alerts mean. That is a real training requirement.

Health Hazards

The point of this project is to monitor the pilot's physical condition like breathing, heart rate, signs of hypoxia or fatigue and catch a problem before it affects the flight. So, system safety and health hazard monitoring are basically what the shirt is for.

Human Requirements

First, it must be comfortable and not interfere with existing gear. Pilots already have a harness, G-suit, oxygen system. If this shirt causes any conflict with those, it simply will not get used.

Second, the whole data process needs to fit into the existing workflow. Who gives out the device, who downloads it afterward if that is unclear or adds too many steps, it will not work in practice.

And the most important one I think: the pilot still must be the one making decisions. Right now, this system can send an alert, but it cannot tell you exactly what is wrong or what to do. The final call stays with the human. I think that boundary is important and it is also an HSI requirement.

How This Relates to HSI Practices

What I think is most important about this lecture is not just what the team built. It is how they approached building it.

They did not start with technology. They started with the people. Before they ran any experiments, they asked whether pilots would use the device and whether it could fit into the real environment. That is the most important step, and it is also the step that a lot of projects skip. A product cannot just work in a lab. It must work in the real world, with real people, in real conditions. If it cannot enter the actual workflow, then it does not matter how well the technology performs.

Then the next step was proving whether the product is useful, not just technically, but from the human side. Data is not automatically valuable. Just because a shirt can collect physiological signals does not mean those signals are telling us something real. The team had to validate the data against medical-grade sensors and prove the readings were reliable under real flight conditions.

And then the most important logic of all, if you want to use data to explain human state, you cannot define the result first and then force the data to match. You need to build a standard first define what different signals mean and then use that standard to interpret the data. That is what the team did when they defined different breathing patterns before trying to assess pilot condition. It is human centered. And that is why I think this project is one of the clearest examples of HSI in practice.

HSI Requirements Generation
and Evaluation of a Product

Course · HSE 225 Institution · Arizona State University Type · HSI Requirements Analysis Subject · Oura Ring Gen 1–4

Part 1 — Introduction

1.1 Goal Definition

Oura Ring is a smart health monitoring wearable designed to help users track their physiological data through continuous sensing. The device collects data in a visualized format through a companion mobile application, allowing users to monitor health metrics, receive early warnings of potential health risks, and make improvements to their daily habits and overall wellbeing. The device provides tracking across more than 50 health indicators, including sleep quality, heart rate, body temperature, blood oxygen saturation, daily activity levels, stress levels, cardiovascular health, and respiratory rate.

1.2 User Description

Oura Ring is primarily designed for everyday consumers, who make up more than 95% of its user base. A small portion of users come from commercial applications, including corporate wellness programs, healthcare institutions, and military organizations, accounting for approximately 5% of total usage. The US military is currently Oura's largest enterprise client, with tens of thousands of service members using the ring for fatigue monitoring.

Oura Ring's core user group consists of individuals between the ages of 25 and 44 who are highly educated, have disposable income, live in urban areas, and have a strong awareness of their personal health. These users are typically early adopters of wearable technology. Following the introduction of women's health features including menstrual cycle prediction in late 2022 and pregnancy insights in 2024, the female user base grew rapidly. By June 2024, female users surpassed male users for the first time, with women accounting for 59% and men accounting for 41% of the user base.

1.3 Product Description

Oura Ring has undergone three major upgrades since its launch in 2015 and is currently in its fourth generation. Generation 1 (2015) focused on sleep stage analysis, readiness scoring, and activity tracking. Generation 2 (2018) improved sleep tracking accuracy, added a body temperature sensor and step tracking, and introduced the Heritage and Horizon design styles. Generation 3 (2021) added 24/7 continuous heart rate monitoring, blood oxygen saturation tracking, more precise temperature sensing, and women's menstrual cycle prediction. Generation 4 (October 2024) introduced Smart Sensing 2.0 technology, increasing signal pathways from 8 to 18 and LEDs from 4 to 10. New features include Cardiovascular Age and VO2 Max estimation. The design was upgraded to full titanium construction.

Generation 4 specifications: Width 7.9 mm, thickness 2.88 mm, weight 3.3 to 5.2 grams. Available in sizes 4 through 15 in six finishes. Battery life 5 to 8 days. Water resistance 100 meters. Pricing starts at $349 with a $5.99/month subscription.

Part 2 — Requirements Definition

HFE — Requirement 1: Wearability and Physical Comfort

Oura Ring requires users to wear it continuously throughout the day and night, including during sleep, exercise, and everyday activities. This requirement evaluates four dimensions: weight, comfort, presence, and impact on daily activities.

Threshold: Weight shall not exceed 6 grams. After 24 hours of continuous wear, SUS comfort score shall be no lower than 68/100. At least 78% of users shall report no longer noticing the ring after 2 hours of wear.

Objective: Weight should not exceed 5.2 grams. SUS score after 7 days ≥ 80. At least 90% of users should be able to completely ignore the ring's presence.

HFE — Requirement 2: Application Interface Usability

Users can only access all their health data through the app. The app must allow users to get started quickly, with all features easy to locate and overall navigation intuitive.

Threshold: New users shall complete device pairing, account setup, and viewing their first health report within 20 minutes without a manual. Task completion rate ≥ 78%. App SUS score ≥ 68.

Objective: Complete above tasks within 10 minutes. Task completion ≥ 90%. SUS ≥ 80.

HFE — Requirement 3: Size Accommodation Range

Finger sizes vary significantly across individuals, and the range of available sizes directly determines how broad a user population the product can serve. An ill-fitting ring causes sensor contact problems that directly affect data accuracy.

Threshold: Shall offer at least 10 size options covering the 5th to 95th percentile finger circumference range. An official sizing tool shall be provided.

Objective: 12 or more sizes covering 1st to 99th percentile. Oura Ring 4 offers sizes 4–15 (12 options), meeting the objective.

Personnel — Requirement 4: Multi-User Group Adaptability

Oura Ring serves users across a wide range of ages, occupations, and technical skill levels. The app should allow for personalized customization — female users may want to prioritize cycle-related data while male users would not need that section. Displaying everything to everyone creates unnecessary information overload.

Threshold: Shall support normal use by individuals aged 18 to 75. App interface shall comply with WCAG 2.1 Level AA accessibility standards.

Objective: WCAG 2.1 Level AAA compliance with specialized guidance for different occupational groups and meaningful personalization.

Training — Requirement 5: Device Setup and Subscription Management

Users must complete Bluetooth pairing, set up a paid subscription, learn how to cancel the subscription if desired, and understand basic troubleshooting. Real-world user feedback indicates that subscription management is one of the most frequently reported problem areas.

Threshold: App shall provide a complete onboarding tutorial covering pairing, subscription activation, cancellation, and troubleshooting. Task completion ≥ 78%.

Objective: Video tutorials and live support. Task completion ≥ 90%, full setup ≤ 15 minutes.

Training — Requirement 6: Data Literacy and Interpretation Support

Oura Ring tracks more than 50 health indicators, which is far too complex for most everyday users to interpret on their own. Misinterpretation of health data can lead to poor health decisions.

Threshold: App shall provide clear explanations for each metric. Users without medical background shall achieve comprehension accuracy ≥ 78% for core metrics.

Objective: Personalized, actionable recommendations based on each user's own data. Comprehension accuracy ≥ 90%.

Health Hazard — Requirement 7: Skin Contact Safety

Oura Ring is in direct and continuous contact with the skin over extended periods of time. The ring's materials may cause allergic reactions, skin irritation, or dermatitis, with particular attention to metal-sensitive users.

Threshold: Shall pass ISO 10993-1 biocompatibility testing, including skin irritation and sensitization testing under ISO 10993-10. Shall contain no known skin-sensitizing metals.

Objective: Validated through independent third-party dermatological clinical testing confirming no accumulative adverse skin reactions after 90 days of continuous wear.

Health Hazard — Requirement 8: Electromagnetic Radiation Safety

Oura Ring contains a Bluetooth Low Energy module. Because the ring is worn on the finger rather than the wrist, it sits in especially close contact with body tissue.

Threshold: Shall obtain FCC certification with SAR not exceeding 1.6 W/kg. Shall also obtain EU CE certification and comply with ICNIRP guidelines.

Objective: SAR below 0.8 W/kg, verified by an independent third-party laboratory with publicly accessible results.

System Safety — Requirement 9: Device Temperature Control

In December 2025, multiple users reported incidents of overheating and emitting smoke, with at least one user sustaining a blister. For a device worn in direct and continuous contact with skin, strict temperature control is essential.

Threshold: Outer surface temperature shall not exceed 43°C under all normal operating conditions (IEC 62368-1). App shall alert user within 5 minutes of abnormal temperature rise.

Objective: Outer surface temperature should not exceed 40°C. Device should automatically reduce power consumption and log the event for review.

System Safety — Requirement 10: Data Collection Accuracy and Reliability

In conditions such as heavy sweating, ring rotation, or improper wear, accuracy may decrease. The system must assess data reliability and proactively inform the user when reliability falls below acceptable levels.

Threshold: Heart rate accuracy ≥ 95%, body temperature ≥ 90%, sleep stage classification ≥ 75%. App shall notify user to check wearing position within 5 minutes of detected reliability drop.

Objective: Heart rate ≥ 99%, temperature ≥ 92%, sleep staging ≥ 79%. System should distinguish data anomalies caused by physiology vs. device issues.

Survivability — Requirement 11: Operation in Extreme Environments

Users may wear the device in extreme cold outdoors, high heat environments such as saunas, heavy sweating during intense physical activity, and water exposure during swimming or showering.

Threshold: Operate normally across −10 to 54°C. Water resistance rated to at least 100 meters. Under heavy sweating, detect changes in data reliability and notify the user.

Objective: Data accuracy across full temperature range should remain ≥ 90% of performance under normal conditions. Continuous operation at depths up to 30 meters underwater.

Part 3 — Evaluation & Conclusion

Summary of Findings

Based on 3,685 real user reviews on Best Buy, the ring received a rating of 4.7 out of 5, with 93% of users willing to recommend it. Positive feedback consistently highlights accurate sleep tracking, comfortable and lightweight wear, long battery life, a discreet appearance, and useful health insights. Negative feedback centers on the ring feeling slightly bulky, subscription payment difficulties, and occasional sizing challenges.

Revenue exceeded $500 million in 2024, more than doubling year over year. This growth was driven primarily by sustained investment in women's health features and successive hardware improvements reducing weight and expanding the size range.

Key Shortcomings

The system lacks a proactive mechanism to alert users when data accuracy has decreased due to sweating, ring displacement, or improper wear. Exercise heart rate tracking is still less accurate than dedicated sports watches. Subscription management transparency and the cancellation flow generated widespread user frustration and led to class action litigation in late 2025, alleging violations of consumer automatic renewal disclosure regulations.

Recommendation 1: Proactive Data Reliability Alert

It is recommended that the system uses real-time cross-metric analysis to assess data quality and alert users within the app to check their wearing position when sensor contact issues are detected. This improvement requires additional algorithm development investment and will introduce some additional battery consumption. Given the potential risk of users being misled by inaccurate health data, this investment is worthwhile and would meaningfully improve user trust in the product.

Recommendation 2: Subscription Design Transparency

Recommended improvements include displaying subscription terms prominently during the purchase flow, adding a direct and easily accessible cancellation option within the app, and offering a free basic feature tier so users can experience the product's value before committing to a paid subscription. Oura Ring is a product that only delivers its full value through long-term continuous use. Once users establish a habit and accumulate personal health data history, they are unlikely to abandon the product. Lowering the initial barrier is therefore more likely to produce long-term loyal users than to increase churn.

How Humans Perceive
Humanoid Robot Movement

Course · HSE 225 Institution · Arizona State University Type · Research Paper Focus · Upper-Limb HRI

Introduction — The Key Question

When people look at robotics, especially humanoid robotics, they very easily take "more human-like" as a sign of success. If it looks more human, people often think it is more advanced, more successful, or simply better designed. But the original point of robotics is not to look human. The point of robotics is to complete tasks and, more importantly, to serve humans. No matter if it is in living scenarios, companion robotics, service robotics, or industrial scenarios, the core is the same: robots are interacting with humans.

So the key is not only movement, and it is not only function. The key is interaction. And once the key becomes interaction, then the key becomes understanding, prediction, and trust. People need to understand what the robot is doing, predict what it will do next, and decide whether they can trust it. If people do not feel comfortable when they use or interact with a robot, then even if the robot completes the task, the interaction is still not successful.

This paper is not mainly about whether robots can complete movement. This paper is about why some robot movements feel natural, while others feel strange. More specifically, this paper focuses on humanoid robot upper-limb movement, including the shoulder joint, elbow joint, wrist joint, palm, fingers, and grasping motion.

Why Human-Likeness Matters — and Why It Creates Problems

Humans are most familiar with themselves. Even if you are not a psychologist, not an anatomist, not a human factors researcher, and not a roboticist, you are still familiar enough with your own body and with other humans around you. You may not be able to describe exactly how each part of the body moves, or what the exact movement limit is, but when you see movement, you immediately know whether it feels human or not. People are also naturally very good at predicting human movement. That is why making robots more human-like can help people understand them more easily.

But the problem also starts here. Robots are still machines. Their systems are built for function, and their systems are fundamentally different from humans. Robots are electrically driven. Humans are biologically driven. Humans move with muscles, joints, tendons, and body coordination. Robots move with motors, actuators, controllers, and mechanical constraints. So even if a robot looks human, it still cannot move exactly like a human. When something looks human, people expect it to move like a human. But when it moves in a way that does not follow human movement logic, people begin to feel strange, uncomfortable, uncertain, and sometimes even scared.

This problem shows up especially clearly in robot arms and hands. Humans use their arms and hands constantly, and humans also watch arms and hands constantly. Upper-limb movement is visually present all the time. People do not spend that much time watching other people's feet, but people spend a lot of time watching hands — hands reaching, turning, pointing, touching, holding, and grasping. So people are extremely familiar with hand and arm movement, including trajectory, speed, timing, and gesture. That is why even a very small unusual thing — like a trajectory that is too straight, movement that is too stiff, or motion that starts too suddenly — can immediately feel wrong.

How Humans Perceive Movement

When people see something move, it does not have to be human, and it does not even have to look human. People do not simply see that its position changed. They immediately begin to judge what it is, whether it is alive, what it is trying to do, and whether it will affect them. So human perception of movement is not passive observation. It is a very active and very sensitive judgment system.

Humans are naturally very sensitive to movement because movement itself carries meaning. When something moves, people do not only see motion. They read intention from motion. This is why theory of mind matters here — humans naturally try to interpret movement as intention. Even simple motion can be read as chasing, avoiding, hesitating, approaching, or threatening.

This is also why biological motion is so important. Humans are extremely sensitive to biological motion. Even if people only see a few moving points in darkness, they can still tell whether it is a person walking, running, reaching, or turning. This shows that humans do not need full form to understand movement. Humans can read movement patterns directly.

Lu and Sperling (2001) explained that human motion perception works through multiple systems at the same time, including brightness-based motion, texture and contrast-based motion, and attention-based motion tracking. This matters because when people watch a robot move, they are not only tracking position. They are also reading edges, material, speed, rhythm, and visual salience all at once. So before people decide whether a robot is efficient, intelligent, or useful, they have already decided whether its movement feels natural.

Problem 1 — When It Looks Human but Doesn't Feel Human

The first problem is when a robotic body part looks human, but does not feel human. For example, it has a palm, five fingers, finger joints, and maybe even skin-colored soft material. It looks like a hand. But when people get closer, they notice the surface is too smooth, too shiny, too cold, too hard, or too rubber-like. Then the problem starts. It looks like a hand, but it does not feel like a real hand.

This is what Poliakoff et al. (2018) discussed in their mismatch hypothesis. They showed that when an artificial body part gives some cues that look human, but other cues do not match human expectation, people can feel uneasy. It is not exactly fear. It is more like something feels wrong. People may not be able to explain it immediately, but they can feel that the signals do not match.

This is also why a fully mechanical industrial arm usually does not create the same reaction. It is clearly a machine. People do not expect it to feel human. So people judge it like a machine. But once something has human shape, people begin to judge it with human standards.

Problem 2 — When Appearance and Motion Don't Match

The second problem is more obvious. Once a robot has a human face, human arms, human hands, and human body proportions, people naturally begin to match it with human movement patterns. They expect it to move in a way that at least feels close to human movement logic. But when it does not, people immediately feel something is wrong.

Wu et al. (2024) showed exactly this point. More anthropomorphic robots can attract more attention, but they can also create more discomfort and uncanny feeling when their appearance and behavior do not match. The more human-like the robot looks, the more human-like people expect it to move.

A human does not simply raise an arm in a straight constant-speed line. Before movement starts, there is usually preparation. The eyes move first. The head may slightly turn. The shoulder adjusts. Then the shoulder joint leads the upper arm, the elbow follows to extend or bend, the wrist adjusts orientation, and the fingers prepare for the target. Human movement is not one joint moving. It is coordinated movement.

But many humanoid robots do not move like this. They start directly. The arm rises in a straight line at constant speed. There is no preparation, no natural coordination, and no gradual transition. So people do not feel like they are watching a human-like arm move. They feel like they are watching a machine execute a command.

This is especially obvious in the wrist. Human wrists are flexible, but they still have natural limits. A human wrist does not suddenly rotate 180 or 360 degrees at high speed. If a humanoid robot does that, even if it is mechanically useful, people immediately feel that something is wrong. Not because they are analyzing the mechanism, but because their body already knows that movement is not normal. The same is true for fingers — human grasping has order: hand approaches, palm adjusts, fingers prepare, thumb and fingers gradually close. But many robot hands close all fingers at once, like a clamp. The action may work, but it does not feel human.

Zaraki et al. (2017) also support this point. They showed that in human–robot interaction, people do not only watch whether the robot completes the action. They also continuously read posture, gesture, direction, distance, and movement as social signals. So once a robot is built in human form, its movement is no longer only mechanical. It is already social.

Problem 3 — Motion Continuity

A lot of robot movement feels wrong not because the robot fails, but because the movement is not continuous. It starts too suddenly, stops too suddenly, changes speed too abruptly, or different joints move out of sync. Human motion perception depends on continuity. Humans naturally connect what they saw one moment ago with what they see the next moment. They automatically judge whether it is the same object and the same continuous action. So when movement is broken, people immediately feel it.

This is also why humans can understand movement from just a few points of light, and why humans can even read intention from moving triangles and dots. Humans are extremely sensitive to motion patterns and naturally assign continuity and intention to movement. So when robot movement is not continuous, people do not see one smooth action. They see broken steps: shoulder moves, pause, elbow moves, pause, wrist turns, fingers close. It feels less like a body and more like a program running step by step.

From an engineering view, the robot may still complete the task. But from a human view, the movement feels unnatural, uncomfortable, and harder to trust.

Conclusion — The Matching Principle

When humanoid robotics make people feel strange, sometimes it is not because they are not advanced enough, not flexible enough, or not human-like enough. The key problem is often much simpler: they move the wrong way. They may look human, but the material does not feel human. They may look human, but the movement does not feel human. They may complete the action, but the movement logic, speed, coordination, and continuity do not match what humans are familiar with.

So the key is not only whether the robot can move, and not only whether it can complete the task. If a robot is meant to interact with humans, especially as a humanoid robot, then movement must match human perception. That is the core point of this whole paper. The key is matching. Not only function, but matching. Not only movement, but movement that matches how humans naturally see, predict, and understand motion.

Project · Architecture — Housing

City Villa

With the population rising, usable land is limited and apartments still dominate the market — yet most people yearn for a villa. This project asks: what if the villa didn't have to move to the suburbs? Individual house-units interlock vertically in the city center, each keeping its own courtyard, so the roof of one home becomes the private terrace of the next.

Duration

Jul – Sep 2023

Type

Studio Design · Personal Work

Site

Shanghai City Center

Structure

Frame + Stacked Courtyard Units

Research — Villa vs. Apartment

From Shanghai's architectural history, the value of individual space keeps shrinking — population growth has made land tight and prices high, while traditional courtyard housing shows private space can still coexist within a shared block. Comparing a river apartment (¥6–14M for 100–200m², near the financial center) against a suburban villa (250–300m², cheaper per square meter but far from the center) found the real gap isn't size — it's control over space, and what that space signals.

Comparison — living space, price, facilities, distance, status
Hypothesis — Circulation Threaded Through Stacked Massing

Before any unit-by-unit derivation, the working hypothesis: a single ribbon of circulation could thread vertically through a loose stack of house-shaped volumes, so that going home is itself a route through shared and private space rather than a single elevator ride.

Project · Architecture — Hospitality

Mind Healing
Restaurant Design

Continuous work cycles quietly break people down — low efficiency, poor focus, a long recovery period. This restaurant is designed as a shelter for students caught in exactly that state: through sound, cold, wind, and light, it redirects attention away from mental fatigue and toward nature, so people can get efficient relaxation in a short amount of time.

Duration

Oct – Dec 2023

Type

Studio Design · Personal Work

Structure

Undulating Shell Roof, Glass Perimeter

Program

Two-Level Dining + Terrace

Research — Vitality Under Continuous Work

On a regular schedule, people stay fully energized — a normal vitality curve rises and falls with rest. Long, uninterrupted work produces a different curve: low efficiency, impaired thinking, and a long repair period afterward. The bottleneck isn't sleep, it's information processing capacity — so the question becomes whether a designed space can reshape that curve directly.

Research
Regular rhythm · space-adjusted curve · continuous-work breakdown
Mind Map

Long-term continuous work splits into two failure modes: low efficiency in the process (not feeling well, lack of thinking ability) and a long repair period after work (severely impaired energy, sleep taking up too much time) — both rooted in the same fundamental element, information processing capacity. Repairing it means reducing the brain's involuntary thinking and relaxing muscle tension; promoting it means stimulating logical-reasoning areas — both in response to students who are demanding of themselves under heavy schoolwork pressure.

Inspires Space

Built on the idea of virtual and real focus in photography: treat an assumed center in the space as the real focus, and the visitor as the virtual one. Reducing the visitor's sense of belonging creates the unknown — and asks whether they could fight it, and get something new from doing so.

Duration

Jan – Feb 2024

Type

Studio Design · Personal Work

Material

Method

Photography Analysis
01

Virtual vs. Real Focus

The real focus is the assumed center of the frame; the visitor becomes a virtual focus, off to the side, uncertain of where they stand.

02

Before / After

Before entering, the space reads as one complete, huge, unknown building. After entering, that unknown collapses — so the design has to work to preserve the fear.

03

Color Extraction

Extracting color from two reference photographs showed that a small amount of bright color inside a large field of dark color triggers a sense of the unknown.

04

Domain Line

A short, sharply transitional space compresses the experience of crossing from known to unknown into just a few seconds.
